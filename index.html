What Is This Session?

In Session 1 you turned your handwriting into a font you could type with. This session goes further. You will turn your hand-made marks into a set of tools you can type onto a page — to connect images, add meaning, and tell the story of your research.

These marks will do real work on the page: linking images together, guiding the reader's eye, framing important details, and showing the relationship between your own photographs or observations (primary research) and the artists or references you have studied (secondary sources).

Punctuation Has Always Been Visual

Before you make your marks, consider this: punctuation has a long history as image, character, and visual language — long before anyone thought to use it as a design tool.

Research Context — Punctuation as Visual Language
Punctuation Personified, 1824 An early children's book that gave each punctuation mark a body, a personality, and a role. The comma bows; the exclamation stands upright. Even then, punctuation was understood as expressive form — not just grammar.
Nicholas Rougeux — Between the Words Chicago designer Rougeux removed all the letters from literary classics, leaving only the punctuation. He then arranged these marks into spirals. The visual rhythm of a whole book becomes visible. Your marks will do something similar — reveal the structure and energy beneath the surface of a page.
Your marks continue this When you draw into a punctuation box, you are not replacing a symbol — you are reinventing it. The function of the mark (direct, connect, pause, emphasise) stays. The form becomes yours.

The Key Idea: Calligraphr as a Mark Library

The idea: Calligraphr does not know what you draw in each box. It just converts any black mark into a character you can type. So instead of writing letters, you draw your own marks, arrows, and textures — and each one is assigned to a key on the keyboard.
  • Press A → your diagonal slash mark appears on the page
  • Press B → your ink splatter mark appears
  • Press C → your gestural underline appears
  • You can make these marks any size — small for fine details, very large for bold effects
  • They work in Photoshop, InDesign, and any other Adobe app

What Makes a Mark Work on a Page?

→ Directional Leads the eye from one image to another
╱ Divider Separates zones while creating energy
◻ Frame Isolates or highlights part of an image
≈ Texture Adds visual rhythm between images
∿ Connector Links primary to secondary research
✱ Emphasis Draws attention to a key idea or source

💡 The most important question

Before you draw any mark, ask: does this mark feel like my research theme? A mark about decay should feel different from a mark about structure. The marks carry meaning before anyone reads a word.
HOMEWORK — Make Your Mark Library

Do this before class. You are designing a set of hand-made marks that will become your visual vocabulary for this project.

Step 1 — Identify Your Research Theme

Your marks should respond to your research focus — the concept or idea driving your work. Look at your research and ask yourself:

Finding Your Theme

  • What connects your artist references? What do they share?
  • Is there a feeling, texture, or quality in your primary research images?
  • If your project had a material quality — rough, fragile, geometric, organic — what would it be?
  • What word would you use as a subheading on your research page?
Example theme Industrial decay / urban memory
Mark quality Scratchy, fragmented, diagonal tension
Avoid Generic neat arrows that could belong to anyone's project

Step 2 — Plan Your Mark Set

You need at least 8–10 marks — enough to cover different functions on the page. Plan one mark for each role:

→ 1–2 arrows For connecting images — make them feel like your theme
╱ 1–2 dividers Lines or strokes that create zones on the page
◻ 1 frame mark A drawn rectangle or enclosure gesture
≈ 2–3 textures Repeated marks, hatching, stipple, grain
✦ 1 emphasis Star, splash, or burst to flag key references
∿ 1 connector A flowing or jagged link between two ideas

Step 3 — Download the Calligraphr Template

Getting Your Template
1
Log in to Calligraphr

Go to calligraphr.com and log in with your Session 1 account.

2
Download the Punctuation Template
  • Click "Templates"
  • Select the punctuation / special characters set
  • Download as PDF and print on white paper
Why punctuation? Each symbol already suggests a mark function — > directs, { } frames, ! emphasises, / divides. Use these as a starting point, then develop them in your own way. A student researching organic forms might turn > into a leaf gesture. Someone researching structure might make { } into a rigid architectural bracket. The punctuation gives you a function — the form is entirely yours.
3
Assign Marks to Boxes

Write a small pencil note at the top of each box — which mark goes here? For example: box ! = emphasis splash, box / = diagonal divider. This is your mark map — keep it, you will need it later.

Step 4 — Make Your Marks

Use black ink on the template. Your marks should fill the box boldly — not timidly. Think about what materials respond to your theme:

Example of a completed mark template — bold, dark marks assigned to character boxes

✓ Works well with Calligraphr

  • Bold brush marks in black ink
  • Thick pen gestures and strokes
  • Stamp impressions (dark ink)
  • Block print marks
  • Drawn shapes — rectangles, arrows, spirals
  • Splattered or flicked ink (bold, not too fine)

⚠ May need testing first

  • Very fine pencil or light charcoal
  • Extremely dense crosshatching
  • Photographic or tonal textures
  • Very pale or diluted marks
Key principle: Calligraphr traces the outline of whatever black shape it finds in each box. It does not know if it is a letter or a mark — it just converts the shape. This means your mark needs to be dark, clear, and kept inside the box. A pale or very faint mark may not be detected.

💡 Make extras

  • Make 2–3 versions of each mark — choose the best one to scan
  • Test on a scrap piece of paper first
  • Wait for all ink to dry fully before scanning
  • Make all marks in the same session so they feel consistent as a set

Step 5 — Scan Your Template

  • Scan at 300 DPI or higher
  • Or use Adobe Scan / Microsoft Lens on your phone
  • Save as JPEG
  • Check it is straight and clearly lit — no shadows across boxes

TASK 1 — Upload & Install Your Mark Font

You are now going to turn your scanned marks into an installable font using Calligraphr. Once installed, you can type your marks into any Adobe app.

Steps to Follow
1
Upload Your Scan
  • Log in to Calligraphr
  • Go to "My Fonts" → "Upload Template"
  • Choose your JPEG file
2
Review Your Upload
  • Look at each box — can you see your mark clearly?
  • Check no marks are cut off at the edges of the box
  • Check the mark fills the box — not too small, not overflowing
3
Check the Quality

Calligraphr has improved significantly — in most cases the app handles cleanup automatically and you will not need to edit individual characters. Simply check that:

  • Each mark has been detected correctly in its box
  • No marks are clipped at the edges
  • The overall set looks consistent
Good to know: The roughness and irregularity of your marks will be preserved — this is what makes them feel hand-made rather than digital.
4
Name and Build Your Font
  • Give your font a name — include your name and theme: e.g. "Sam_Industrial_Marks"
  • Click "Build Font"
  • Check the preview — does it look right?
5
Download and Install
  • Download the .ttf or .otf file
  • Mac: Double-click the file → click "Install Font"
  • Windows: Right-click the file → click "Install"
  • Important: Close and reopen Photoshop or InDesign after installing
6
Test Your Marks
  • Open Photoshop or InDesign
  • Select the Text tool — find your font by name in the font list
  • Type the punctuation keys you assigned to your marks
  • Your marks should appear — try making them bigger and smaller

💡 Size is everything

Because these are font characters, you change their size using the font size field. A mark at 12pt is a small detail. The same mark at 200pt becomes a large graphic element that fills part of the page.

TASK 2 — Build a Research Layout Page
Words to know for this task:
  • Primary research — images, photographs, or observations you made yourself
  • Secondary sources — images, artworks, or references made by other artists or designers that you have researched
  • Knowledge text — a short paragraph where you explain what you understand about your research topic, in your own words
  • Visual narrative — the way your page guides the reader through your ideas using images, marks, and layout — telling a story without needing to explain it out loud
  • Reference — a credit next to an image that tells the reader who made it, when, and where you found it

Now use your mark font to build a research page that tells the story of your project. This is not about decoration — every mark should be doing a job.

What Your Page Must Include

Primary research Your own photographs, sketches or observations
Secondary sources Artist references, historical images, archive material
Knowledge text A short paragraph of written analysis in your own words
Source references Artist name, date, or institution for every image not your own
Your marks Used to connect, frame, direct, and narrate

Layout Principles

Look at Micky Zhuo's work again — these are the decisions that make the pages work:

╱

Use a diagonal

A strong diagonal line creates energy and guides the eye across the whole page — from one group of images to another.

⬌

Connect primary to secondary

Use arrows or connector marks to link your own photographs to artist references. Make the relationship between them visible.

◻

Frame to analyse

A drawn rectangle over part of an image is not just decoration — it is saying "look here, this part matters." Use it to analyse.

≡

Layer text as mark

Try rotating text or placing it at an angle. Text can be a graphic element — not just information sitting in a straight line.

●

One accent colour

Choose one colour and use it consistently for one purpose — links, key words, or a connecting thread across the page.

░

Let marks breathe

Do not fill every gap. Empty space between marks and images is part of the composition. Space makes marks feel deliberate.

Type on a Path in Photoshop

One of the most powerful ways to use text as a mark is to run it along a path — around a shape, a silhouette, or a freehand curve. Here is how:

Type on a Path — Quick Guide
1
Draw Your Path
  • Select the Pen tool (P) or Ellipse tool
  • In the top toolbar, set the mode to "Path" — not Shape or Fill
  • Draw a curved line, a circle, or trace around a silhouette
2
Attach Text to the Path
  • Select the Type tool (T)
  • Hover over your path — the cursor changes to show a wavy line through it
  • Click on the path and start typing — your text will follow the curve
3
Adjust Position
  • Use the Path Selection tool (black arrow) to drag the text along the path
  • Flip text to the inside of a curve by dragging it through the path line
  • Change the font size to make the text larger or smaller
4
Hide the Path
  • Go to View → Show → Target Path to hide the path line
  • Or simply click elsewhere — the path will not show when you export or print

💡 Around a silhouette

To run text around a figure or object, use the Pen tool to trace its outline as a path, then attach your text. The text becomes part of the composition — it describes, questions, or echoes the image it surrounds.

Referencing Sources

Every image on your page that is not your own work must be credited. This shows the depth of your research and is required for all submitted work.

How to Reference on a Layout Page

  • Artist name + work title + year — place this close to the image, small and consistent
  • If from a website: artist name + website name is enough
  • If from a book or archive: author or institution + publication name
  • Use your accent colour for reference text — as Micky did — to make it visually consistent and easy to find
  • References are part of the design — not an afterthought added at the end

Writing Knowledge on the Page

Your page should include at least one piece of written analysis — not just a caption, but a short paragraph that shows you understand the context of your research.

💡 What "knowledge text" means

  • Not just a label: "This is a photo of a cracked wall."
  • But an explanation: "The cracked surface in this photograph connects to my research into urban decay — how buildings record time, damage, and human use on their surfaces."
  • Try to explain: what do you see? Why does it matter to your project? How does it connect to an artist or idea you have researched?
  • This text can be placed at any angle or size on the page — but it must still be easy to read
